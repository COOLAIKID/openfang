## FRAMEWORK 1: CURIOSITY HEADLINES (4 headlines)

Curiosity headlines create an information gap — the reader must click to fill it. The best ones tease a counterintuitive truth or a surprising element the reader didn't know to look for.

Rules for this framework:
- Hint at the answer without giving it away
- Create tension between what they believe and what is true
- Avoid false curiosity (the payoff must deliver what the tease promised)
- Work best for: blog posts, email subject lines, social media, YouTube

**Curiosity formulas:**
- "The [surprising thing] About [topic] Nobody Talks About"
- "What [authority/expert/company] Doesn't Want You to Know About [topic]"
- "Why [common belief] Is [surprisingly] Wrong"
- "The [unexpected element] That Changes Everything About [topic]"

## FRAMEWORK 2: BENEFIT-LED HEADLINES (4 headlines)

Benefit headlines make a specific, credible promise. They answer "what's in it for me?" immediately. The mistake most people make is writing feature headlines instead — "introduces new X" vs. "finally get Y."

Rules for this framework:
- Lead with the outcome, not the product or method
- Be specific: numbers, timeframes, and named results outperform vague promises
- Address the real desire, not the surface want ("lose weight" → "fit into your college jeans again")
- Work best for: ads, landing pages, sales pages, email subject lines

**Benefit formulas:**
- "[Achieve Specific Result] Without [Common Drawback]"
- "[Do X] in [Timeframe] — Even If [Objection]"
- "Finally: A Way to [Achieve Desire] That Actually [Works/Sticks/Lasts]"
- "[Specific Outcome] Guaranteed — or [Risk Reversal]"

## FRAMEWORK 3: HOW-TO HEADLINES (4 headlines)

How-to headlines signal immediate, actionable value. They tell the reader they will learn to DO something, not just know something. These are Google's favorite headlines because they match high-intent search queries.

Rules for this framework:
- Be specific about the method or approach ("How to..." is weak; "How to [specific method] to [specific result]" is strong)
- Include a timeframe or qualifier to increase credibility
- Address a specific person or situation when possible
- Work best for: SEO articles, tutorials, YouTube thumbnails, email subject lines

**How-to formulas:**
- "How to [Achieve Specific Result] in [Timeframe] Without [Sacrifice]"
- "How [Type of Person] Can [Achieve Outcome] Even With [Limitation]"
- "How I [Achieved Result] — and How You Can Too"
- "How to [Do X] the Right Way (Most People Do It Wrong)"

## FRAMEWORK 4: LISTICLE HEADLINES (4 headlines)

Listicle headlines promise organized, scannable value. They reduce reader anxiety ("I won't have to read a wall of text") while promising completeness. The number does heavy lifting — pick it carefully.

Rules for this framework:
- Odd numbers outperform even numbers in most tests (7, 9, 11, 13)
- Use an adjective before the number or noun: not "10 Tips" but "10 Proven Tips"
- The topic noun should be specific, not vague ("ways to grow" → "Instagram growth tactics")
- Add a benefit phrase after the list topic: "...That Will [Outcome]"
- Work best for: blog posts, newsletters, social media, roundup articles

**Listicle formulas:**
- "[Number] [Adjective] [Methods/Tips/Strategies] to [Achieve Outcome]"
- "[Number] Things [Target Audience] Do Differently (And Why It Works)"
- "[Number] [Adjective] Ways to [Solve Problem] — Even If [Objection]"
- "The [Number] [Topic] Secrets That [Authority] Use But Never Teach"

## FRAMEWORK 5: QUESTION HEADLINES (4 headlines)

Question headlines engage the reader's mind before they've chosen to read. A good question headline creates a "yes" response or a "I've wondered that too" recognition. Bad question headlines let people answer "no" and move on.

Rules for this framework:
- Never write a question readers can easily answer "no" to without losing anything
- The question should trigger self-recognition: "Is that me?"
- Open questions outperform closed questions: "How would X change Y?" beats "Do you want X?"
- Use "you" to make it personal
- Work best for: email subject lines, ad headlines, landing page subheads, social posts

**Question formulas:**
- "Are You [Making Mistake / Missing Out / Doing X Wrong]?"
- "What Would Your [Life/Business/Health] Look Like If [Desired Outcome]?"
- "Why Are [Successful People/Category] [Doing Surprising Thing]?"
- "Is [Common Belief/Practice] Actually [Holding You Back/Wrong/Costing You]?"
